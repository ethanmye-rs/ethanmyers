Refractory Mixes

I’m looking to build a kiln to experiment with dichroic glasses and case hardening steel, and need a work envelope of around 30L. Most kilns intended for pottery or glass tempering are far too large, or require 220V. Given the premium on space and lack of easily available single phase 220, I’ve opted to build and design my own kiln.

I’ll be using an Ikea bathroom cabinet with an interior volume of 58L uninsulated. This provides for an convenient form factor and aesthetically pleasing case to hold brittle refractory.

I’ve also opted to design and build my own refractory. Generally speaking, doing things from scratch is a surefire way to turn a simple project into an impossible one, but in this case, there’s a good reason. Commercial refractory mix is expensive, mostly for the difficulty inn moving a 50lb bag of rock. most ingredients are common silicates and oxides, there are generally no fancy ingredients.

However, given this apparent simplicity, there is a surprising amount of FUD on the Internet on just exactly how a good refractory mix is made, what constitutes a good mix, and how it should be validated.

For my design, I’ve decided to pursue two separate avenues for an insulating material, as well as a kiln linear. The first is a traditional porous aggregate, held together by a sodium silicate binder. The second is a carbon foam, produced by dehadration of a simple sugar or something akin to starlite

Refractory mixes, silicates

Mix 1

    11.9g Vermiculite
    8.1g fireclay
    23.4g binder, 37.5% wt sodium metasilicate

    Heated till water left
    left to cure overnight
